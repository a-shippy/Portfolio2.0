"There are so many busses, I don't know which one to catch!"

ABOUT

I was given a prompt with an already identified issue. With recent additions to routes, there was an influx of buses to certain bus stops and riders were not able to decipher which one they were supposed to take. In order to find the best solution, I had to identify user needs and frustrations with already-existing transit applications.

CONSTRAINTS

The timeframe for this project was tight, therefore a reasonable solution had to be decided on quickly. This project also required me to code the solution so it had to be within my capabilities.

SOLUTION

Provide a screen on an existing platform that helps bus riders locate their route, which bus is on that route, how to recognize it, and how estimated time of arrival and the total time of the trip. Find the bus they need to be on and access all of its necessary information. By interviewing users and bus drivers, conducting surveys, analyzing systems already in place, and other research and development tools, a plan of action was established.

RESEARCH

Understand public transit.

USER
SURVEY

Find out what demographics are, or were, using the bus or are not using it and why.

The user survey was utilized to get insight from those who have used public bus transportation. It was vital to ask those who have used public bus systems what they experienced to find pain points. From these surveys and follow up interviews, the problems began to reveal themselves, allowing for a plan of action in eradicating them.

USER
STORIES

Ensure all necessary actions for catching a bus were accounted for.

The user stories were crafted towards new and returning public transit goers with a concentration on people traveling. This solution needed to cater to someone who might be completely unfamiliar with a given area and bus system.

Looking back, getting more insight from bus drivers who interact with riders daily would have provided a helpful perspective. I would spend more time in contact with them to see what insights they could provide.

IDEATE

Brainstorm solutions for transportation accessibility.

FIRST
SKETCH

Lo-fi sketches are an inexpensive step that allows pain points to be worked out.

Upon opening the app, the first screen would be the map showing the current location as well as the closest bus stop with bus information. There are radio buttons to select the bus out of those available at the stop. The next screen is the fastest route to the particular destination searched (from the first screen). The user must choose to start or view more routes to the location. This screen is missing either a back button or 'X' in the search field. The third screen shows other routes with bike stations as a second mode of transportation. This screen is targeting users who enjoy the newer options of bike and scooter sharing.

Post-testing, I didn't find that users were confused but the problem wasn't being solved for and scope creep had trickled into the designs.

SECOND
SKETCH

Reflect back on user stories and focus on the high priority tasks.

This set of sketches start with a welcome screen that provides little information or options to avoid overwhelming the user. It first prompts the user if they'd like to go straight to the nearest bus stop. If not, they can choose to go home and have an option to search there. The second screen is the nearest bus stop selection. There is a list of incoming buses and the time of arrival. After selecting the bus, the next screen is the bus route information.

This solved the problem of riders not knowing which bus to get on or when they'd be arriving.

DESIGN

Translate ideas into products.

FIRST
WIREFRAME

Make sure the steps to accomplish tasks are clear and understandable.

This part of the process took two iterations with a few adjustments. There weren't many screens to iterate on because I was working on an already-established issue and the user prompts were very quick. I wanted to get user feedback from a digital form of the product because this is more familiar to users and the feedback is more accurate.

DESIGN
SOLUTION

Design an intuitive screen that is familiar for new and returning users.

For the design, I wanted bold colors that were familiar in the world of commute. These are all colors we unanimously see on the road. The hierarchy differentiates the information needed. All that is included on the screen are bus numbers, bus destination, time of arrival, and their status. If more information is needed, a new screen will open. This was done to reduce the amount of content being digested.

As I ran usability tests, I found that accessibility wasn't completely accounted for and needed refining. Along with that, the design needed some fine-tuning to make it modern and clean.

ITERATE

Test the product and refine.

SECOND
WIREFRAME

Listen to the users.

The first high-fidelity design mockup went through testing and after consideration, the design needed to be cleaned up for usability reasons. The new design was more modern and concise. All color accessibility issues were eliminated. More distinguishable branding was also introduced.

FINAL
DESIGN

Listen to the users.

TAKEAWAY

Lessons to take into the next project.

The most glaring struggles I had with this project was scope creep. In other words, I let my creative ideas get ahead of the goals. Trying to incorporate newer additions to public transit like bike and scooter shares, this definitely provided a unique feature to transit apps. However, this did not solve the problem riders were having at bus stops that were overwhelmed with new routes and buses.

By testing, reiterating, and redesigning, I was able to develop a product with a solution that followed accessibility guidelines.
